# Calories Burned Hiking: Complete Guide by Weight, Distance, and Elevation

**Hiking burns 400–900 calories per hour** depending on your body weight, trail difficulty, elevation gain, and whether you're carrying a pack. That makes hiking one of the highest-calorie-burning outdoor activities available — significantly more than flat-surface walking and competitive with jogging.

This guide covers exactly how to calculate calories burned hiking, reference tables by weight and distance, and how different factors affect your total calorie burn.

## How Hiking Calorie Burn Is Calculated

All calorie estimates for hiking use the **MET (Metabolic Equivalent of Task)** method — the same standard used by sports scientists and researchers worldwide:

```
Calories = MET × Weight (kg) × Time (hours)
```

Hiking MET values range from **5.3 to 9.0** depending on terrain and intensity, compared to 3.5–5.0 for flat walking. This is why hiking burns so many more calories than a regular walk at the same pace.

Here are the MET values for common hiking scenarios:

| Hiking Type | MET Value |
|-------------|-----------|
| Flat trail, light pace | 5.3 |
| Cross-country, moderate terrain | 6.0 |
| Moderate hills, steady pace | 6.5 |
| Steep terrain, significant elevation | 7.0 |
| Backpacking with heavy pack | 7.8 |
| Mountain hiking, difficult terrain | 8.0 |
| Very steep climbing, rocky terrain | 9.0 |

For comparison, flat-surface walking MET values run 3.5–5.0. Hiking's higher MET reflects the added demands of uneven terrain, elevation changes, and lateral balance adjustments your muscles make constantly on a trail.

## How Elevation Gain Affects Calorie Burn

Elevation gain is one of the biggest variables in hiking calorie burn. Climbing uphill dramatically increases energy expenditure:

| Elevation Gain | Additional Calories per 1,000 ft Gained (155 lbs) |
|----------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| Gradual grade (~5%) | ~80–100 cal |
| Moderate grade (~10%) | ~130–160 cal |
| Steep grade (~20%) | ~200–240 cal |
| Very steep (~30%+) | ~280–350 cal |

A hike with 2,000 feet of elevation gain burns roughly 300–500 additional calories compared to a flat hike of the same distance. This is why mountain hikes are so calorie-intensive despite sometimes covering less total distance than flat trail hikes.

The descent matters too — hiking downhill burns about 30–40% fewer calories per mile than uphill, but still more than flat terrain because of the eccentric muscle work required to control descent.

## Backpack Weight and Its Effect on Calorie Burn

Carrying a loaded backpack increases calorie burn proportionally to the added weight:

| Pack Weight | Calorie Increase vs. No Pack |
|-------------|------------------------------|
| 10 lbs | +10–12% |
| 20 lbs | +18–22% |
| 30 lbs | +27–33% |
| 40 lbs | +35–42% |
| 50+ lbs | +45–55% |

A 155-lb person hiking with a 30-lb backpack burns approximately 27–33% more calories than the same hike with no pack. For a 3-hour moderate hike that would normally burn 900 calories, adding a 30-lb pack brings that total to roughly 1,150–1,200 calories.

This is why backpacking trips are among the most effective activities for calorie burn per day — many thru-hikers burn 4,000–6,000 calories daily on long trails.

## Trail Difficulty Levels and Expected Calorie Burn

Most trail rating systems use difficulty levels that directly correlate with calorie burn:

**Easy trails** (flat to gentle grade, good footing): MET 5.3–6.0
- Example: paved rail trails, flat forest paths
- Calorie burn for 155 lbs: 370–420 cal/hour

**Moderate trails** (some elevation, mixed terrain): MET 6.0–7.0
- Example: regional park trails with rolling hills
- Calorie burn for 155 lbs: 420–490 cal/hour

**Difficult trails** (sustained elevation, rocky terrain): MET 7.0–8.0
- Example: mountain trails with significant elevation gain
- Calorie burn for 155 lbs: 490–560 cal/hour

**Strenuous trails** (steep, technical, scrambling): MET 8.0–9.0
- Example: ridge hikes, summit approaches with scrambling
- Calorie burn for 155 lbs: 560–630 cal/hour

When planning a hike for calorie burn, always check the trail's elevation profile alongside total distance. A 5-mile trail with 2,500 feet of gain burns far more than a flat 5-mile trail.

## How to Estimate Calories for Your Specific Hike

Follow these steps for a reliable calorie estimate before your hike:

1. **Find your trail's difficulty rating and elevation gain** from AllTrails or a similar source
2. **Select the appropriate MET value** from the table above
3. **Estimate your hiking time** based on Naismith's Rule: 3 miles per hour + 1 hour per 2,000 feet of elevation gain
4. **Apply the formula**: Calories = MET × your weight in kg × hours
5. **Add 10–30% if carrying a pack** using the pack weight table above

**Example:** A 180-lb (82 kg) person hiking a moderate-difficult 6-mile trail with 1,500 feet of elevation gain, no pack:
- Estimated time: ~2.5 hours (2 miles/hour accounting for elevation)
- MET value: 7.0 (moderate-difficult terrain)
- Calories: 7.0 × 82 × 2.5 = **1,435 calories**

## FAQ

### How many calories does hiking burn per mile?

A 155-lb person burns approximately 100–150 calories per mile hiking on moderate terrain. Steeper trails and heavier packs push that higher. On flat trails, it's closer to 80–100 calories per mile — similar to walking.

### Is hiking good for weight loss?

Yes, hiking is excellent for weight loss. A 2-hour moderate hike burns 700–900+ calories for most adults — equivalent to a significant calorie deficit. Hiking 3–4 times per week, combined with a healthy diet, can produce 1–2 lbs of weight loss per week.

### Does hiking burn more calories than walking?

Significantly more. Hiking burns 40–80% more calories than flat-surface walking at the same pace because of trail elevation changes, uneven terrain, and lateral stabilization demands. A moderate 60-minute hike burns roughly 420 calories vs. 300 calories for flat walking at the same duration.

### How accurate are fitness trackers for hiking calories?

Most wrist-based fitness trackers underestimate hiking calories by 20–40% because they don't account for elevation gain or pack weight. GPS-enabled devices that measure elevation are more accurate. For best results, use the MET-based calculation above with your actual weight and trail data.

### Does hiking uphill burn more calories than hiking downhill?

Yes. Uphill hiking burns approximately 60–70% more calories per mile than downhill. However, downhill hiking still burns more than flat walking because of the eccentric muscle contractions required to control descent — particularly in the quadriceps.

### What should I eat before a long hike to support calorie burn?

For hikes under 2 hours, a normal meal 2–3 hours beforehand is sufficient. For hikes over 3 hours, bring 200–300 calories of carbohydrates per hour (energy bars, trail mix, fruit) to maintain energy and fat-burning efficiency.
