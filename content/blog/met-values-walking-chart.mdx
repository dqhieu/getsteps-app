# MET Values for Walking: Complete Chart and Calorie Guide

Ever wonder why a brisk walk feels so much harder than a casual stroll — and whether the calorie difference is worth picking up the pace? The answer lies in MET values. MET values for walking give you a science-backed way to compare exercise intensity and calculate exactly how many calories you burn at any speed.

Whether you are trying to lose weight, plan workouts, or simply understand what your fitness tracker is calculating behind the scenes, this guide breaks down every MET value for walking — from a leisurely 2.0 mph stroll to competitive race walking — in plain language with a practical chart you can reference anytime.

## What Is a MET Value?

MET stands for **Metabolic Equivalent of Task**. It measures how much energy an activity requires compared to sitting quietly at rest.

- **1 MET** = the energy you burn sitting still, which is roughly **1 calorie per kilogram of body weight per hour** (about 3.5 mL of oxygen per kg per minute).
- **2 METs** = twice the energy of sitting still.
- **5 METs** = five times the energy of sitting still.

Think of METs as a universal intensity score. A MET value of 3.5 means that activity burns 3.5 times more energy than resting — regardless of who is doing it. The values come from the **Compendium of Physical Activities**, a research database maintained by Arizona State University that catalogs the energy cost of hundreds of activities.

MET values for walking range from about 2.0 (very slow walking) to 8.0+ (race walking and steep uphill hiking), making walking one of the most versatile exercises for controlling workout intensity.

## How to Calculate Calories from MET Values

The standard MET calorie formula is straightforward:

```
Calories Burned = MET × Weight (kg) × Time (hours)
```

That's it. Three numbers multiplied together. Let's walk through two examples.

### Example 1: Brisk Walking for 30 Minutes

- **Activity:** Walking 3.5 mph (MET value brisk walking = 4.3)
- **Weight:** 155 lbs (70 kg)
- **Duration:** 30 minutes (0.5 hours)

```
Calories = 4.3 × 70 × 0.5 = 150.5 calories
```

### Example 2: Walking 3 mph for 1 Hour

- **Activity:** Walking 3.0 mph (MET value walking 3 mph = 3.5)
- **Weight:** 180 lbs (82 kg)
- **Duration:** 60 minutes (1 hour)

```
Calories = 3.5 × 82 × 1.0 = 287 calories
```

### Example 3: Uphill Hiking for 45 Minutes

- **Activity:** Hiking, hills with a daypack (MET = 7.5)
- **Weight:** 140 lbs (64 kg)
- **Duration:** 45 minutes (0.75 hours)

```
Calories = 7.5 × 64 × 0.75 = 360 calories
```

If you know the MET values, you can design walks that hit a specific calorie target. Want to burn 300 calories in 45 minutes? The formula tells you that a 155 lb person needs an activity around MET 5.7 — meaning a brisk pace on a slight incline will do the job. ## MET Values for Walking vs. Running

How does walking compare to running in terms of metabolic equivalent? Here is a side-by-side comparison:

| Activity | Speed | MET Value |
|----------|-------|-----------|
| Walking | 3.0 mph | 3.5 |
| Brisk walking | 3.5 mph | 4.3 |
| Very brisk walking | 4.0 mph | 5.0 |
| Jogging | 5.0 mph | 8.3 |
| Running | 6.0 mph | 9.8 |
| Running | 7.0 mph | 11.0 |

The jump from fast walking (MET 5.0) to jogging (MET 8.3) is significant — but the gap between brisk walking and slow jogging is smaller than most people expect. For anyone who finds running hard on the joints, [brisk walking on a treadmill incline](/blog/treadmill-walking-workout-weight-loss) can match jogging-level calorie burn at a fraction of the impact.

## Tips for Using MET Values Effectively

**Start with your baseline.** Weigh yourself in the morning and convert to kilograms (divide pounds by 2.205). This is the number you'll plug into the formula.

**Use brisk walking as your benchmark.** A MET value of 4.3 (brisk walking at 3.5 mph) is the sweet spot where you get meaningful calorie burn without overexertion. Most health organizations define "moderate exercise" as 3.0–6.0 METs.

**Add incline for a bigger burn.** Walking at 3.5 mph on flat ground is MET 4.3, but add a 6% incline and it jumps to MET 8.0 — nearly doubling your calorie expenditure. This is one of the most time-efficient ways to burn more [calories per step](/blog/calories-burned-per-step).

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is 1 MET equivalent to?

One MET equals the energy your body uses while sitting quietly — approximately 1 calorie per kilogram of body weight per hour, or 3.5 milliliters of oxygen per kilogram per minute. For a 155 lb (70 kg) person, 1 MET equals about 70 calories per hour. Every MET value is a multiple of this baseline, so an activity rated at 4 METs burns four times as much energy as resting.

### What is the MET value for brisk walking?

The MET value for brisk walking at 3.5 mph (5.6 km/h) is **4.3**. This is classified as moderate-intensity exercise by the American College of Sports Medicine. Walking slightly faster at 4.0 mph raises the MET to 5.0, which borders on vigorous intensity for some individuals.

### What is the MET value for walking at 3 mph?

The MET value for walking at 3 mph (4.8 km/h) is **3.5**. This is a comfortable, moderate pace — fast enough to count as exercise but slow enough to hold a conversation. It is one of the most commonly referenced MET values for walking because 3 mph is an average adult walking speed.

### How many METs is 10,000 steps?

It depends on your walking speed. If you cover 10,000 steps at a moderate 3.0 mph pace, the activity is rated at 3.5 METs. At a brisk 3.5 mph, it is 4.3 METs. The MET value stays the same regardless of step count — it measures intensity, not volume. To estimate calories from steps, use our [Steps to Calories Calculator](/tools/steps-to-calories-calculator).

### Are MET values accurate for everyone?

MET values are population averages based on research data. They provide a reliable estimate for most adults but may slightly overestimate calorie burn for very fit individuals (who are more efficient at a given pace) and underestimate for beginners or those carrying extra weight. For the most accurate personal tracking, a wearable with heart-rate monitoring combined with MET-based formulas gives the best results.
