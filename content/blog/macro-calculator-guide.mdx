# Macro Calculator Guide: Protein, Carbs and Fat Targets

Counting calories gets you part of the way to your nutrition goals — but tracking macros gets you the rest. Macronutrients (protein, carbohydrates, and fat) are the three building blocks of your diet, and the ratio in which you consume them determines whether you lose fat, build muscle, or simply maintain your current weight. A macro calculator takes the guesswork out of this process by translating your body weight, activity level, and goal into specific daily gram targets. This guide explains how macros work, how to calculate yours, and how to hit your targets consistently.

## What Are Macros?

Macronutrients are the nutrients your body requires in large quantities to function. There are three:

- **Protein** — 4 calories per gram. Builds and repairs muscle tissue, supports immune function, and keeps you full longer than any other macro.
- **Carbohydrates** — 4 calories per gram. Your body's preferred energy source. Fuels the brain, muscles, and virtually every biological process.
- **Fat** — 9 calories per gram. Regulates hormones, supports brain health, and enables absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K).

Each macro plays a distinct role. Cutting any one of them entirely is rarely a good strategy — it's the ratio and total quantity that matter for your specific goal.

## How to Calculate Your Macros

Getting your macro targets right is a three-step process.

**Step 1: Calculate your TDEE.** Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) is the number of calories your body burns each day, accounting for your basal metabolic rate and activity level. Use the [TDEE Calculator](/tools/tdee-calculator) to get your baseline number.

**Step 2: Choose a goal.** Are you trying to lose body fat, maintain your current weight, or build muscle? Each goal requires a different calorie target and macro split. Weight loss typically means eating 300–500 calories below TDEE; muscle gain means eating 200–400 calories above it.

**Step 3: Apply macro ratios.** Once you have your calorie target, divide it across protein, carbs, and fat using goal-appropriate percentages (see the table in the next section).

## Macro Ratios by Goal

Different goals call for different macro distributions. Here are the most widely used evidence-based splits:

| Goal | Protein | Carbohydrates | Fat |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weight Loss | 40% | 30% | 30% |
| Maintenance | 30% | 40% | 30% |
| Muscle Gain | 30% | 45% | 25% |

These percentages are starting points, not rigid rules. If you find yourself feeling sluggish on a lower-carb split, add more carbs and reduce fat slightly. If you're not recovering well between workouts, increase protein. Nutrition is iterative — adjust based on how your body responds over 2–4 weeks.

## How Much Protein Do You Need?

Protein is the most important macro to nail, especially if body composition is your goal.

**For muscle building:** 1.6–2.2g of protein per kilogram of body weight per day is the range supported by the majority of exercise science research. For a 75kg person, that's 120–165g of protein daily.

**For general health and weight management:** 1.2–1.6g per kg is sufficient. This level still preserves muscle mass during a calorie deficit and keeps hunger well-controlled.

Protein has the highest thermic effect of food (TEF) — your body burns roughly 20–30% of the calories from protein just during digestion. It also triggers satiety hormones more effectively than carbs or fat, making it your best tool for managing hunger on a deficit.

## Carbs: How Many Do You Need?

Carbohydrates have been unfairly demonized, particularly by low-carb diet culture. The reality: carbs are your body's most efficient fuel source, and eliminating them entirely impairs both physical performance and cognitive function for most people.

**For performance and muscle gain**, higher carb intakes (45–55% of calories) support glycogen replenishment, training intensity, and recovery. **For weight loss**, moderate carb intake (30–40%) works well alongside high protein and adequate fat.

Timing matters too. Consuming most of your carbohydrates around your workouts — 1–2 hours before or within 2 hours after — maximizes their use for energy and recovery, rather than storage. Whole food carb sources (oats, sweet potato, rice, fruit) should make up the bulk of your intake.

## Healthy Fats: Don't Skip Them

Fat is not the enemy. It is calorie-dense (9 cal/g), so it requires portion awareness, but cutting fat too low causes real problems: disrupted hormone production, poor brain function, and deficiency in fat-soluble vitamins.

Aim for 20–35% of your daily calories from fat. Focus on quality sources:

- **Monounsaturated fats:** olive oil, avocado, almonds
- **Polyunsaturated fats (Omega-3):** salmon, sardines, walnuts, flaxseed
- **Saturated fats (in moderation):** eggs, dairy, coconut oil

Avoid **trans fats** entirely — found in partially hydrogenated oils and many processed snack foods. They raise LDL cholesterol and lower HDL cholesterol, increasing cardiovascular risk with no nutritional benefit.

## Best Foods for Each Macro

Building meals around whole foods makes hitting your macro targets significantly easier. Here's a reference table:

| Macro | Top Food Sources |
|---|---|
| Protein | Chicken breast, eggs, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, lentils, tuna, tofu |
| Carbohydrates | Oats, brown rice, sweet potato, quinoa, banana, whole grain bread, legumes |
| Fat | Olive oil, avocado, almonds, walnuts, salmon, chia seeds, full-fat dairy |

Many foods contain multiple macros — eggs provide protein and fat, lentils provide protein and carbs. Your macro calculator gives you gram targets, and you build meals from these foods to hit those numbers.

## How to Track Macros (Practical Tips)

Tracking macros precisely takes a little setup, but it becomes second nature within 1–2 weeks.

**Use a food scale.** Volume measurements (cups, tablespoons) are inconsistent. Weighing food in grams gives you accurate macro counts, especially for calorie-dense foods like nuts and oils.

**Meal prep reduces tracking friction.** When you cook in batches, you weigh once and track once — then eat the same meal multiple times without re-entering data.

**Start with protein.** Hit your protein target first when planning meals. Then fill in carbs and fat around it. This approach ensures your most important macro is covered before the smaller adjustments happen.

**Track for 2–4 weeks.** You don't need to track macros forever. A focused tracking period teaches you portion sizes and macro content of your regular foods — knowledge that sticks long after you stop logging.

## FAQ

**What are good macros for weight loss?**
A 40/30/30 split (protein/carbs/fat) works well for most people in a calorie deficit. High protein (40%) preserves muscle mass and controls hunger, moderate carbs (30%) maintain energy, and fat (30%) supports hormones. The calorie deficit itself drives fat loss — macros determine body composition during that process.

**Is counting macros better than counting calories?**
Macro tracking is more informative than calorie counting alone. Two diets can have identical calorie totals but very different macro breakdowns, producing different results for body composition, muscle retention, and satiety. Tracking macros automatically tracks calories, so you get both.

**How much protein do I need per day?**
For muscle building, aim for 1.6–2.2g per kg of body weight. For general health or weight management, 1.2–1.6g per kg is sufficient. A 70kg person building muscle should target roughly 112–154g of protein per day.

**What is a 40/30/30 macro split?**
A 40/30/30 split allocates 40% of daily calories to protein, 30% to carbohydrates, and 30% to fat. It's a high-protein approach commonly used for weight loss and body recomposition. At 2000 calories, that's 200g protein, 150g carbs, and 67g fat per day.

**Do macros matter if I'm in a calorie deficit?**
Yes — significantly. A calorie deficit determines whether you lose weight, but your macro split determines whether that weight is fat or muscle. Eating sufficient protein (1.6g+ per kg) while in a deficit is the most evidence-backed strategy for losing fat while preserving lean mass.
